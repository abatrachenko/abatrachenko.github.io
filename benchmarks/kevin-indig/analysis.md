# Kevin Indig

**URL:** https://www.kevin-indig.com
**Captured:** 2026-04-23
**Category:** Solo-expert / SEO consultant (closest direct comp to resonanceseo.com)

## Why this site

Kevin Indig runs the closest peer practice to Aleksey's: a senior solo SEO/organic-growth advisor to enterprise and venture-scale brands (Ramp, Nextdoor, Snap, Dropbox, Reddit, Toast; ex-Shopify, ex-G2, ex-Atlassian), paired with a large newsletter ("Growth Memo," 26,000+ subscribers) that functions as both credibility and top-of-funnel capture. The business model we are designing for — consulting income subsidized by a research-led newsletter that doubles as a first-touch lead magnet — is precisely his model. The homepage is a useful study in how that dual engine gets staged: how much real estate the newsletter gets vs. the consulting CTA, and how thought-leadership proof (testimonials from Heads of Growth, subscriber count) is recruited to validate the advisory offer.

## Hero

- Headline approach: **name + what** — "I unlock Organic Growth." First-person, category-naming, no outcome promise, no numbers. Minimal and declarative. Preceded by "Kevin Indig - Organic Growth Advisor" as positioning wordmark.
- Subhead strategy: **category-definition** — expands the category ("Organic Growth strategies that cover SEO, CRO, brand strategy and Product-Led Growth") and name-drops operator-past ("hiring, org design"). Does not carry a stat.
- CTA: **"Work with me"** — single primary CTA, button treatment, routes to an anchor contact form on the same page. No newsletter CTA above the fold on the homepage itself; the "Growth Memo" link sits in the top nav instead.
- Supporting visual: image-led hero (portrait + title lockup). Warm, editorial-feeling rather than SaaS-product screenshot.

## Proof

- Social proof format: **logos + named testimonials + subscriber count + follower count**. All four are used.
- Placement:
  - Client logos (Nextdoor, Snap Inc., Bounce, Ramp, Riverside FM, Toast, Reddit, Dropbox) sit in a logo bar mid-page.
  - Five named testimonials, each with full name + role + company: Damien Beard (Nextdoor), Amanda Butler (EzLicence), Sri Batchu (Ramp), Nikita Frampton (Bounce), Connor Sullivan (Toast). Every attribution is a Head of Growth / Head of Marketing / Director-level buyer — mirroring his ICP back at visitors.
  - A numeric line appears inside the "Why work with Kevin" block: *"Every week, Kevin shares industry research with over 25,000 newsletter subscribers and 59,000 LinkedIn followers."* Two distribution numbers in one sentence, load-bearing work.
- What makes it credible: every testimonial is from a buyer at the tier Kevin sells to. None are from peers or influencers. Two numbers (subscribers + followers) and a logo row carry the "he's read" argument without needing a personal-brand flex.

## Offer structure

- How they package what they sell: one offer, named "Advisory," with a secondary "Workshop" option surfaced in the contact form dropdown. A "Problems I help clients solve" list (7 items) and a "Tangible outcomes" list replace conventional service tiers. There are no named packages, no hour bundles, no duration.
- Pricing visibility: **none.** No pricing tier, no hourly rate, no retainer range, no "starting at." Every qualification happens in the contact form (First name, Last name, Email, Company name, Advisory/Workshop dropdown, Subject, Message).

## FAQ / objections

- No dedicated FAQ section. Objection handling happens implicitly through:
  - "Why work with Kevin" block (3 pillars: unique operating experience, horizontal + vertical benchmarking, deep industry intelligence) — each neutralizes a specific alternative (agencies without operator experience, narrow consultants, generalists).
  - Testimonials explicitly pre-empt objections ("ramped up extremely quickly," "translate complex data into actionable recommendations" handle the "will this be useful fast?" concern).
- Dodged: pricing, engagement length, availability, what's explicitly out of scope. No anti-positioning statement on site.

## Design system observations

- Type: **sans-serif throughout**, medium-weight, relatively large headlines. Editorial-feeling rather than SaaS-techy. Section headings shipped as image assets (`Why work with Kevin.png`) suggesting Webflow/Wix-style layout with typographic treatments baked into images.
- Color: restrained neutral palette with warm accent. Not glossy SaaS blue. Reads closer to a journalist/analyst site than an agency site.
- Motion: light / minimal. Not motion-forward.
- Density: **airy.** Single-column rhythm. Each proof block (logos, testimonials, advisory pillars) gets its own band with generous whitespace. Low information density per screen height.

## What we'd steal

- **Single-category H1 in first person ("I unlock Organic Growth")** — structural cousin of our locked direction. It names the category, puts the operator (not the firm) at the grammatical subject, and refuses to front-load a benefit. Our variant: keep "Senior SEO + AI search leadership for enterprise e-commerce — embedded directly in your team" as H1 but borrow Kevin's move of letting the *subhead* do the outcome work ("From organic plateau to compounding growth…"). Fits the locked voice: declarative, no hype, operator-voiced.
- **Testimonial casting by buyer title, not by brand prestige** — every Indig testimonial names a Head of Growth / Head of Marketing / Director. That's the buyer staring back at the visitor. For resonanceseo.com, where Pillar A logos (Adidas, Samsung, J.Crew, Madewell) already carry brand prestige, testimonials should be cast for **title match to our ICP** (VP Marketing / VP E-commerce / Director of E-commerce) over big-name attribution.
- **Subscriber count + LinkedIn followers as one inline authority line** — *"Every week, Kevin shares industry research with over 25,000 newsletter subscribers and 59,000 LinkedIn followers"* — one sentence, two numbers, ambient rather than a CTA. Directly portable to a "15+ years · 35+ brands · $700M+ in organic revenue" treatment that slips into a "Why work with Aleksey" block without becoming a stats-grid cliché.
- **Mid-page 3-pillar differentiator block ("Why work with Kevin")** — three short, numbered reasons that each neutralize a distinct alternative. The structural home for our locked Differentiator Triangle (embedded senior operator / enterprise track record / GEO + SEO). One-for-one pattern match.
- **Editorial layout treatment over SaaS-grid** — airy, one-column-per-section rhythm and restraint on motion signal "analyst, not agency." This is the visual posture we want. It reads as someone whose time is worth money — not someone selling a product.

**Deliberate newsletter-placement divergence.** Kevin's homepage surfaces the newsletter primarily through the nav link ("Growth Memo") and a one-line inline mention of subscriber count — the actual signup lives on the separate growth-memo.com Substack, which presents a Subscribe CTA with three testimonial quotes from SEO peers (Barry Adams, Casandra Campbell, Kirkland Gee). This is the pattern we explicitly **should not** copy. Our Stage 1 positioning calls for the newsletter to be the *primary* above-the-fold capture, not a nav link. The pattern to steal from Kevin is how the Substack landing is structured — tagline-first H1 (*"Growth Memo delivers sharp, research-backed insights on SEO, organic growth, and AI-driven search for senior marketing leaders"*), subscriber count as proof, three peer testimonials, single "Subscribe" button. That compact template is what our in-hero email capture block should look like, collapsed into ~80px of vertical: *headline ("Organic growth patterns for enterprise e-commerce. Every other Tuesday.") + one line of proof + email field + single CTA.* Borrow the Substack component design; reject the burial.

## What we'd leave

- **Hiding pricing entirely + funneling everything through a single long contact form.** Kevin runs pure "contact us" commerce with seven form fields and no pricing signal. For resonanceseo.com this does not fit: our positioning is locked to a **two-layer staged CTA** (newsletter primary, strategy call secondary), with three real pricing tiers ($6,400 / $9,600 / $12,800 per month) and an "Hours Available This Month" scarcity bar. Those tiers and the availability signal filter out sub-$50M brands at the door and pre-qualify by budget before any form fill. Adopting Kevin's opaque, form-wall model would collapse our two-layer CTA system, lose the scarcity proof, and invite misqualified inbound. His opacity works because he sells at startup-board-advisor tier on warm inbound; ours needs to work on cold enterprise buyers who self-qualify on price.

- **Image-based section headings (e.g., `Why work with Kevin.png`).** Bad for accessibility, SEO, and Core Web Vitals — all three matter for an SEO consultant's own site. We ship section headings as real HTML.

- **No explicit anti-positioning.** Kevin never states who he *won't* work with. Our locked positioning requires the opposite: three stated boundaries (not an agency, not for sub-$50M brands, not a generalist). We keep them visible.
