# Linear

**URL:** https://linear.app
**Captured:** 2026-04-23
**Category:** SaaS (product-development tool) — included here strictly as a **design-polish benchmark**, not a positioning or offer comp.

## Why this site

Linear is routinely cited as one of the best-crafted marketing sites on the web. The surface feels calm, expensive, and confident — three attributes we want for an enterprise consultant site where the buyer is a VP/Director of Marketing. Linear sells a SaaS product to engineering teams and we sell embedded SEO leadership to e-commerce leaders, so the offer, proof shape, and CTA strategy do not transfer. What does transfer is the visual-craft vocabulary: disciplined typography, restrained palette, motion that earns its place, and generous but purposeful whitespace. This benchmark is purely a design-quality reference.

## Hero
- Headline approach: **category-name** — "The product development system for teams and agents." Declarative, not outcome-promise. Names the thing.
- Subhead strategy: **expand-promise** — "Purpose-built for planning and building products. Designed for the AI era." One short sentence, calibrated to the headline.
- CTA: "Get started" (primary) and "Contact sales" (secondary) in the header; a tertiary editorial link ("Issue tracking is dead" → /next) sits near the hero as a provocation. Three surfaces, each with a distinct job.
- Supporting visual: high-resolution product UI screenshot, rendered at display scale, sitting directly under the headline with no decorative frame. The UI itself is the hero art.

## Proof
- Social proof format: **customer testimonials + named logos** (OpenAI, Ramp, Opendoor surface mid-page); no star ratings, no "as featured in" strips.
- Placement: mid-page, after the feature narrative rather than above the fold. Linear trusts the product demo to do the first layer of persuasion and uses proof as reinforcement.
- What makes it credible: named individuals at recognizable companies, restrained quote length, no stock-photo headshots styled to look "premium." The design restraint *is* the credibility signal.

## Offer structure
- Packaging: five feature pillars presented as a numbered scroll narrative — **1.0 Intake → 2.0 Plan → 3.0 Build → 4.0 Diffs → 5.0 Monitor**. Each pillar is a full-width section with a name, a single sentence, and a product visual.
- Pricing visibility: linked in nav; not surfaced in the homepage body. Enterprise-ready but not enterprise-opaque.

## FAQ / objections
- No dedicated FAQ block on the homepage. Objections are pre-empted by the feature narrative (each section answers an implicit "does it actually do X?"). Changelog and customer quotes further handle recency and trust objections without a Q&A format.

## Design system observations
- **Type:** All sans-serif. Inter (or a very close grotesque) used across hero, body, and UI. Hero headline sits at ~72–96px desktop at bold weight (700–900). Body copy sits at a comfortable reading size with generous line-height. No serif pairings, no display-font flourish, no italics used for emphasis. One family, many weights.
- **Color:** Light background dominant on the marketing pages, with a highly restrained palette — near-black text, off-white surfaces, and a single desaturated blue as the brand accent. Product screenshots carry their own (darker) UI palette, which gives the page visual variety without requiring the marketing layer to introduce new colors. Gradients are rare and subtle. Three or four meaningful colors total.
- **Motion:** Subtle fade-and-rise reveals on scroll; gentle hover lifts on buttons; no parallax, no marquee, no auto-playing video noise. Product visuals may include looping micro-animations inside the UI frame, but the page chrome itself stays still. The restraint is the signature.
- **Density:** Airy. Large vertical gaps between numbered sections create a pacing that reads as "each of these matters." Hero is deliberately uncrowded — headline, subhead, product shot, that's it. Nav is thin and minimal.
- **Navigation:** Six top-level items (Product, Resources, Customers, Pricing, Now, Contact), a Product dropdown for the five pillars, and auxiliary links (Docs, Open app, Log in, Sign up) on the right. No search, no mega-menu, no sticky promo bar.

## What we'd steal

- **One-family typographic system with aggressive weight contrast.** Linear runs its whole marketing surface on a single grotesque (Inter-class) and does all the expression through size and weight. For a solo-operator consulting site this is a cheat code: it eliminates the font-pairing decision that most consultant sites get wrong (serif display + sans body almost always reads "personal brand") and signals enterprise-tier craft without custom type licensing. Translation to resonanceseo.com: Inter at a display size and bold weight for the H1 ("Senior SEO + AI search leadership for enterprise e-commerce — embedded directly in your team."), the same family at regular weight for body, nothing else.

- **Disciplined palette — one neutral surface, one accent, product/work visuals carry the color.** Linear limits the marketing-page palette to three or four meaningful colors and lets screenshots introduce any additional hue. For a consultant site, the equivalent is: off-white surface, near-black type, one accent color used only for CTAs and link underlines, and let the client-logo row and any case-study data visualizations carry the rest. This reads as confident rather than branded.

- **Whitespace as hierarchy.** The gap between sections on linear.app is wider than most sites would dare, and it's the single largest contributor to the "expensive" feel. On a consulting site with less content to stage, this translates directly: pad the hero, pad between proof blocks, let the Adidas/Samsung/J.Crew/Madewell logo row breathe, and resist the instinct to fill vertical space with filler text.

- **Motion that exists to confirm, not to perform.** Fade-and-rise reveals at ~200ms with minimal translate distance. No parallax, no scroll-jacking, no headline word-by-word animation. Every motion serves either "this element just entered view" or "this button is interactive." For a research-led, technical-plainspoken voice, this restraint *is* the voice expressed in motion.

- **Category-naming H1 over benefit-claim H1.** Linear's "The product development system for teams and agents" is structurally identical to our locked positioning line — it names the thing and lets proof carry the outcome. Validates the position-first choice already made in `conversion/positioning.md`.

## What we'd leave

- **The five-pillar numbered scroll narrative (Intake → Plan → Build → Diffs → Monitor).** It works for Linear because they have a product with five self-contained surfaces to screenshot. A consultant doesn't have product surfaces — we have engagements and outcomes. Transplanting the scroll-narrative structure would force us to invent visuals for each pillar, which would push us toward either stock illustration or fabricated dashboards. Both would undercut the senior-operator voice. Our proof shape (client logos → named outcomes → case studies → bio) is a different pattern and should stay that way.

- **Product-UI screenshots as primary hero art.** No equivalent asset exists for us, and faking one (mocked "SEO dashboard" imagery) would signal agency-slick rather than embedded-senior.

- **In-app micro-animations inside looping UI frames.** Requires design-team capacity to maintain as the product evolves; irrelevant to a consulting offer.

- **Six-item top nav with a Product dropdown.** Linear needs that architecture because they have feature surfaces, docs, changelog, and customers to route to. A consultant site should run a three- or four-item nav at most.
